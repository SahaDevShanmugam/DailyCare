# Medication Safety in Heart Failure

## Common HF medications (summary for patient support)

### ACE inhibitors / ARBs / ARNIs

- **Purpose**: Lower blood pressure, reduce strain on the heart, improve outcomes in HFrEF.
- **Typical frequency**: Once or twice daily.
- **Cautions**: May cause cough (ACE-i), dizziness, high potassium, kidney function changes. Blood tests (potassium, kidney function) are usually checked periodically.
- **Contraindications**: Pregnancy; bilateral renal artery stenosis; history of angioedema with ACE-i; severe hyperkalemia. Care with potassium supplements or salt substitutes high in potassium.

### Beta-blockers (e.g. bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol)

- **Purpose**: Slow heart rate, lower blood pressure, improve survival in HFrEF.
- **Typical frequency**: Once or twice daily; often started low and increased gradually.
- **Cautions**: Can cause fatigue, dizziness, low heart rate, cold hands/feet. Do not stop suddenly.
- **Contraindications**: Severe asthma/bronchospasm (relative); very low heart rate; severe hypotension. Use caution in COPD.

### Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (e.g. spironolactone, eplerenone)

- **Purpose**: Reduce fluid retention and improve outcomes in HFrEF.
- **Typical frequency**: Once daily.
- **Cautions**: Can raise potassium; avoid potassium-rich diet or supplements unless advised. May cause gynecomastia (spironolactone).
- **Contraindications**: High potassium; severe kidney disease. Blood tests (potassium, kidney function) are monitored.

### SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g. empagliflozin, dapagliflozin)

- **Purpose**: Reduce hospitalizations and mortality in HF (HFrEF and in many cases HFpEF).
- **Typical frequency**: Once daily.
- **Cautions**: May increase urinary frequency, risk of genital infections; maintain hydration; watch for dehydration in illness.
- **Contraindications**: Severe kidney impairment (dose/use depends on function); diabetic ketoacidosis (in diabetes).

### Diuretics (e.g. furosemide, bumetanide)

- **Purpose**: Remove excess fluid to relieve shortness of breath and swelling.
- **Typical frequency**: Once or twice daily; sometimes extra dose as needed per care plan.
- **Cautions**: Can cause low potassium, dizziness, dehydration. Take in the morning/afternoon to reduce night-time urination if possible.
- **Contraindications**: Severe low potassium or sodium; anuria. Dose adjusted for kidney function.

### Digoxin

- **Purpose**: Helps control heart rate and symptoms in some HF and atrial fibrillation patients.
- **Typical frequency**: Once daily.
- **Cautions**: Narrow therapeutic range; toxicity can cause nausea, vision changes, confusion, arrhythmias. Blood level monitored.
- **Contraindications**: Ventricular fibrillation; certain arrhythmias; toxicity.

## General safety principles

- **Adherence**: Take medications at the same time(s) each day; use a pill organizer or reminder if helpful.
- **Do not stop** beta-blockers or other HF drugs suddenly; taper only under supervision.
- **Side effects**: Report new or worsening symptoms (dizziness, fatigue, swelling, shortness of breath, confusion) to the care team.
- **Interactions**: Tell the care team about all prescription and over-the-counter drugs and supplements.
- **Salt substitutes**: Many are high in potassium; avoid unless the care team approves.
- **NSAIDs**: Avoid or use only under supervision; can worsen HF and kidney function and interact with diuretics and ACE-i/ARBs.

This is for educational support only; all dosing and suitability must be determined by the care team.
